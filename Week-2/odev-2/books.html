Major Works

Discourse on the Arts and Sciences (1750)

Rousseau’s first major philosophical work, in which he argued that the progress of the arts and sciences had not improved morality. Instead, he believed it had led to the corruption of virtue and the degradation of society.

Discourse on the Origin of Inequality (1754)

In this work, Rousseau explores the development of human society and the emergence of social inequality. He contends that inequality is not a natural condition but a result of social institutions and the rise of private property.

The Social Contract (1762)

One of Rousseau’s most influential works, it introduces the concept of the "general will" and argues that legitimate political authority rests on a social contract agreed upon by all citizens for their mutual preservation.

Emile, or On Education (1762)

A treatise on education and child-rearing, Emile emphasizes the importance of allowing children to develop naturally and learn through experience. It remains a foundational work in the philosophy of education.

Confessions (1782)

A groundbreaking autobiographical work that influenced modern memoir and literature. Rousseau’s openness and introspection in Confessions helped establish new standards for personal narrative.
